# Music Theory Fundamentals
topic: music_theory_basics
difficulty: beginner

## What is Music Theory?
Music theory is the study of how music works. It gives us a shared language to describe what we hear — why a chord sounds tense or resolved, why a melody feels like it needs to go somewhere, why some songs feel joyful and others feel melancholic. Theory doesn't constrain creativity; it explains it. Once you understand the patterns, you can use them intentionally or break them deliberately.

# Intervals
topic: intervals
difficulty: beginner

## What is an Interval?
An interval is the distance between two notes. Everything in music — chords, scales, melodies — is built from intervals. Learning intervals is like learning the alphabet before you read.

The smallest interval in Western music is a **half step** (also called a semitone) — the distance between any two adjacent keys on a piano, like C to C# or E to F. Two half steps make a **whole step** (or whole tone), like C to D.

### Common Intervals (from C)
- Minor 2nd (1 half step): C → C# — sounds dissonant, tense. Hear it in the Jaws theme.
- Major 2nd (2 half steps): C → D — sounds like the first two notes of "Happy Birthday."
- Minor 3rd (3 half steps): C → Eb — the sound of sadness. Think of the opening of "Smoke on the Water."
- Major 3rd (4 half steps): C → E — bright, happy. The first two notes of "Oh When the Saints Go Marching In."
- Perfect 4th (5 half steps): C → F — strong and stable. "Here Comes the Bride."
- Tritone (6 half steps): C → F# — maximum tension, the "devil's interval." Think the opening riff of "Purple Haze" or the Simpsons theme.
- Perfect 5th (7 half steps): C → G — powerful and open. Power chords are built on this. "Twinkle Twinkle Little Star."
- Minor 6th (8 half steps): C → Ab — bittersweet. "The Entertainer."
- Major 6th (9 half steps): C → A — bright and warm. "My Bonnie Lies Over the Ocean."
- Minor 7th (10 half steps): C → Bb — bluesy tension. The sound of a dominant 7th chord.
- Major 7th (11 half steps): C → B — dreamy, unresolved. Think bossa nova and jazz ballads.
- Octave (12 half steps): C → C — same note, higher pitch. "Somewhere Over the Rainbow."

# Scales
topic: scales
difficulty: beginner

## The Major Scale
The major scale is the foundation of Western music. It has 7 notes and follows a specific pattern of whole steps (W) and half steps (H):

**W W H W W W H**

Starting on C: C D E F G A B (C)

This is why C major is the easiest key — all white keys on a piano. Every major scale has the same emotional quality: bright, happy, resolved.

**Song examples of major scale feel:** "Happy Birthday," "Let It Be" (verses), "Don't Stop Believin'," most pop music.

## The Natural Minor Scale
The natural minor scale is the "sad" counterpart to major. Same notes as its relative major, but starting on a different note. The formula:

**W H W W H W W**

Starting on A: A B C D E F G (A) — all white keys, but starts and resolves on A instead of C. This is A natural minor.

**Song examples:** "Stairway to Heaven" intro, "Hotel California," "Scarborough Fair."

## The Pentatonic Scale
Five notes instead of seven. The most universal scale in the world — found in blues, rock, country, folk, and music from nearly every culture.

Major pentatonic: 1 2 3 5 6 (drop the 4th and 7th from major)
Minor pentatonic: 1 b3 4 5 b7 (the blues workhorse)

**Song examples of minor pentatonic:** Basically every rock guitar solo ever — "Smoke on the Water," "Whole Lotta Love," "Pride and Joy." The minor pentatonic is the most played scale in popular music.

## The Blues Scale
Minor pentatonic + one note: the b5 (flat five), also called the "blue note." This note creates that characteristic blues tension and release.

Formula: 1 b3 4 b5 5 b7

That b5 is what gives blues its ache. When B.B. King bends up to that note, that's the blue note.

# Chords
topic: chords
difficulty: beginner

## What is a Chord?
A chord is three or more notes played together. Most chords are built by stacking intervals of thirds (every other note in a scale).

## Triads — The Building Blocks
A triad has three notes: a root, a third, and a fifth.

**Major triad**: Root + major 3rd + perfect 5th. Sounds happy and stable. C major = C E G.
**Minor triad**: Root + minor 3rd + perfect 5th. Sounds sad or serious. C minor = C Eb G.
**Diminished triad**: Root + minor 3rd + diminished 5th. Sounds tense and unstable. C diminished = C Eb Gb.
**Augmented triad**: Root + major 3rd + augmented 5th. Sounds dreamy/unsettled. C augmented = C E G#.

## Seventh Chords
Add one more third on top of a triad and you get a seventh chord. These are where music starts to get interesting.

**Major 7th (Cmaj7)**: C E G B — warm, dreamy, sophisticated. Think bossa nova, "Misty," "The Girl from Ipanema."
**Dominant 7th (C7)**: C E G Bb — tense, wants to resolve. The engine of blues and jazz. "Johnny B. Goode," "Sweet Home Chicago."
**Minor 7th (Cm7)**: C Eb G Bb — smooth, cool, melancholic. "So What" by Miles Davis is built on minor 7th chords.
**Half-diminished (Cm7b5)**: C Eb Gb Bb — dark tension. Common in jazz and film scores as the ii chord in minor keys.

# Harmony and Chord Progressions
topic: harmony
difficulty: intermediate

## Functional Harmony
In tonal music, chords have functions — they create tension and resolution. The three main functions are:

- **Tonic (I)**: Home base. Stable, resolved. You can rest here.
- **Subdominant (IV)**: Departure. Moves away from home, creates gentle tension.
- **Dominant (V or V7)**: Maximum tension. Strongly wants to resolve back to I.

The most fundamental progression in Western music: **I → IV → V → I** (or V7 → I)

## The ii-V-I (the Jazz Foundation)
The most important chord progression in jazz. In the key of C major:
- **ii** = Dm7
- **V** = G7
- **I** = Cmaj7

The ii-V-I is everywhere in jazz standards. "Autumn Leaves," "All The Things You Are," "Misty" — they're essentially chains of ii-V-I movements through different keys. Learn to hear and play ii-V-Is and you've unlocked jazz.

## Common Pop/Rock Progressions

**I-V-vi-IV** (the most overused progression in pop music): C G Am F
Songs: "Let It Be," "With or Without You," "Don't Stop Believin'," hundreds more. Once you hear it, you'll hear it everywhere.

**I-IV-V** (the blues progression): C F G
Foundation of 12-bar blues, rock and roll, country. "Johnny B. Goode," "La Bamba," "Twist and Shout."

**vi-IV-I-V** (emotional pop): Am F C G
Songs: "Someone Like You" (Adele), "Apologize," "Zombie" (The Cranberries).

# Modes
topic: modes
difficulty: intermediate

## What are Modes?
Modes are scales derived from the major scale by starting on different degrees. There are 7 modes, one starting on each note of the major scale. They each have a distinct emotional character.

Using C major (C D E F G A B) as the parent scale:

1. **Ionian** (start on C): C D E F G A B — This IS the major scale. Bright, happy.
2. **Dorian** (start on D): D E F G A B C D — Minor feel but with a raised 6th. Sophisticated, slightly mysterious. "So What" (Miles Davis), "Oye Como Va" (Santana).
3. **Phrygian** (start on E): E F G A B C D E — Dark, Spanish/Flamenco flavor. The flat 2nd gives it an exotic quality. "White Zombie," flamenco music.
4. **Lydian** (start on F): F G A B C D E F — Major scale with a raised 4th. Dreamy, floating, otherworldly. Film scores, "Flying" (Beatles), lots of John Williams.
5. **Mixolydian** (start on G): G A B C D E F G — Major scale with a flat 7th. The rock and roll mode. "Sweet Home Chicago," "Norwegian Wood," most classic rock.
6. **Aeolian** (start on A): A B C D E F G A — This IS the natural minor scale. Sad, serious, introspective.
7. **Locrian** (start on B): B C D E F G A B — Diminished 5th makes it very unstable. Rare in practice; used in metal and jazz for tension.

**The key insight**: Modes aren't just scales — they're flavors. Dorian minor sounds different from Aeolian minor because of that one raised note. Learning to hear the difference between D Dorian and D Aeolian is a real ear training milestone.

# Jazz Harmony — Tritone Substitutions
topic: tritone_substitution
difficulty: advanced

## What is a Tritone Substitution?
A tritone substitution (or "tritone sub") replaces a dominant 7th chord with another dominant 7th chord whose root is a tritone away. Since a tritone is exactly half an octave, every dominant 7th chord has exactly one tritone sub partner.

**Example**: G7 (the V7 in C major) can be substituted with Db7. Why? Because G7 (G B D F) and Db7 (Db F Ab Cb/B) share the same tritone: B and F. The guide tones are the same, just inverted.

## Why It Works
The two most important notes in a dominant 7th chord are the 3rd and 7th — they form the tritone, and their resolution is what creates the pull to the tonic. G7's tritone (B → F) resolves to C major (C → E) the same way Db7's tritone (F → B/Cb) does. Same resolution, different bass note.

The result: instead of G7 → Cmaj7 (root movement down a 5th), you get Db7 → Cmaj7 (root movement down a half step — smooth chromatic bass line).

## Where You Hear It
- The classic ii-V-I with tritone sub: Dm7 → Db7 → Cmaj7 (instead of Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7)
- "Fly Me to the Moon" bridge
- "All The Things You Are" — Parker and Coltrane use tritone subs constantly
- "Giant Steps" (Coltrane) — the entire architecture is built on tritone-related key centers

Once you hear tritone subs, you'll notice them constantly in jazz recordings. The bass moving chromatically into a chord change is almost always a tritone sub at work.
