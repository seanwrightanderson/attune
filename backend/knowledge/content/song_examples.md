# Song Examples by Theory Concept
topic: song_examples
difficulty: all

## ii-V-I Progressions in Songs
The ii-V-I progression is the backbone of jazz harmony. The best songs to hear it in are: **Autumn Leaves** — the entire song is a chain of ii-V-Is cycling through G major and G minor. **Misty** opens with a lush Ebmaj7 and moves through ii-V-Is in a relaxed ballad feel. **All The Things You Are** moves through six key centers in 36 bars, each approached by a ii-V. **Stella by Starlight** begins ambiguously with a half-diminished ii chord before resolving — a ii-V-I in minor. When teaching ii-V-I, start with Autumn Leaves in G major, then show how the same pattern works in minor with the same song's second half.

## Tritone Substitution in Songs
Tritone substitutions replace a dominant 7th chord with another dominant 7th chord a tritone away, creating a half-step bass resolution. **Giant Steps** (Coltrane) is the most extreme example — the entire architecture is built on tritone-related key centers. **Fly Me to the Moon** uses a Db7 substituting for G7 in the bridge, creating a smooth chromatic bass line. **Night and Day** (Cole Porter) has a verse that sits a tritone away from the eventual tonic. For beginners encountering tritone subs, Fly Me to the Moon is the gentlest introduction.

## Modal Jazz Songs
Modal jazz uses scales (modes) as tonal centers rather than chord progressions. **So What** (Miles Davis, Kind of Blue) is the definitive example — D Dorian for 16 bars, Eb Dorian for 8, back to D Dorian. **Impressions** (Coltrane) uses the same structure. **My Favorite Things** (Coltrane) alternates between E Dorian and E major, showing how a mode can sustain improvisation for minutes without harmonic movement. The lesson: in modal jazz, the player creates tension through melody and rhythm, not by navigating changes.

## Dorian Mode in Songs
Dorian is a minor mode with a raised 6th — brighter and more sophisticated than natural minor. **So What** (Miles Davis) is pure D Dorian. **Oye Como Va** (Tito Puente/Santana) vamps between Am7 and D7 — the D major chord (not Dm) is the Dorian signature. **Scarborough Fair** (traditional/Simon & Garfunkel) shows Dorian in a folk context. **Impressions** (Coltrane) is D Dorian for most of its duration. When students ask why Dorian sounds "less sad" than natural minor, point to the raised 6th degree — it's one note, but it changes the emotional color dramatically.

## Mixolydian Mode in Songs
Mixolydian is a major scale with a flat 7th — used heavily in rock, blues rock, and Celtic music. **Norwegian Wood** (Beatles) sits in E Mixolydian, with the D natural appearing in the melody. **Baba O'Riley** (The Who) opens with an F Mixolydian synth arpeggio. **Sweet Home Chicago** and most 12-bar blues use Mixolydian because every chord is a dominant 7th — the flat 7th is structural. When a rock song sounds "almost major but darker," Mixolydian is usually the answer.

## Aeolian (Natural Minor) in Songs
Aeolian is the natural minor scale — the foundation of sad, serious, or introspective music. **Eleanor Rigby** (Beatles) stays in E Aeolian throughout, oscillating between Em and C. **Hotel California** (Eagles) uses Bm as its tonal center with the V7 (F#7) borrowed from harmonic minor. **Stairway to Heaven** (Led Zeppelin) opens in A minor with a chromatic descending bass. **Riders on the Storm** (The Doors) maintains E Aeolian throughout. **Scarborough Fair** (Simon & Garfunkel) shows Dorian vs. Aeolian — compare their 6th degrees to hear the difference.

## Phrygian Mode in Songs
Phrygian has a flat 2nd degree, giving it a Spanish, flamenco, or exotic quality. **White Rabbit** (Jefferson Airplane) is built on a Phrygian bolero and climaxes by resolving the flat 2nd (G) up a half step to the tonic (F#). **Caravan** (Duke Ellington) uses Phrygian dominant — the Phrygian scale with a raised 3rd — in its main vamp. The key teaching point: the half-step relationship between scale degree 2 and the tonic (b2 to 1) is what gives Phrygian its tension and its characteristic resolution.

## Lydian Mode in Songs
Lydian is a major scale with a raised 4th — dreamy, floating, otherworldly. It's prevalent in film scores (John Williams uses it constantly). **Gymnopédie No. 1** (Satie) uses parallel major 7th chords with a Lydian flavor. The raised 4th creates a tritone with the tonic, which gives Lydian its sense of suspension and mystery. When a major key piece sounds unusually "spacious" or "elevated," check for a raised 4th.

## Borrowed Chords (Modal Interchange) in Songs
Borrowed chords come from the parallel minor (or other parallel modes). **Creep** (Radiohead) — the B major chord (III major) is borrowed from parallel Mixolydian, and the Cm (minor iv) from parallel minor. **Yesterday** (Beatles) — the Bb minor/F chord is a minor iv borrowed from F minor. **Stairway to Heaven** has borrowed chords throughout its progression. The emotional effect: borrowed chords introduce darkness into a major key context, or lift a minor key momentarily. The contrast is the point.

## 12-Bar Blues in Songs
The 12-bar blues form uses three chords (I7-IV7-V7) in a specific 12-bar pattern. **Sweet Home Chicago** (Robert Johnson) is a foundational 12-bar blues in E. **Johnny B. Goode** (Chuck Berry) and **La Bamba** both use I-IV-V in a similar feel. **Pride and Joy** (Stevie Ray Vaughan) is a Texas shuffle 12-bar. Every chord is a dominant 7th, even the I chord — this is why blues harmony is always slightly tense. The form itself provides resolution through repetition.

## Minor Blues in Songs
Minor blues substitutes minor chords for the dominant 7th tonic, creating a darker character. **The Thrill Is Gone** (B.B. King) is in B minor blues — Bm7-Em7-F#m7 instead of B7-E7-F#7. **Summertime** (Gershwin) is harmonized as a minor blues. The minor blues sound is more melancholic and sophisticated than major blues.

## Pentatonic Scale in Songs
The pentatonic scale (5 notes) is the most universal scale in popular music. **Smoke on the Water** (Deep Purple) uses parallel perfect 4ths moving through G minor pentatonic. **Whole Lotta Love** (Led Zeppelin) — the riff is pure E minor pentatonic. **My Girl** (Temptations) — the guitar intro is C major pentatonic. **Superstition** (Stevie Wonder) — the clavinet riff is Eb minor pentatonic. For guitarists, the minor pentatonic "box" position is the first shape to learn; for pianists, the black keys form a pentatonic scale.

## Blues Scale in Songs
The blues scale adds a flat 5th (blue note) to the minor pentatonic. **Pride and Joy** — SRV hits that b5 constantly in his bends. **The Thrill Is Gone** — B.B. King's bends often target the blue note. The blue note is the "ache" of the blues — it's neither major nor minor, creating maximum tension before resolving.

## Chromatic Bass Movement in Songs
Chromatic bass lines create smooth forward motion under static or shifting harmony. **Blackbird** (Beatles) — McCartney's bass descends chromatically while the melody floats above. **Fly Me to the Moon** bridge — tritone sub creates a chromatic bass descent. **Billie Jean** (Michael Jackson) — the bass line's chromatic movements define the harmony more than the chords do. **Stairway to Heaven** intro — A descending to G#-G-F# under shifting chords.

## Odd Time Signatures in Songs
Odd meters create rhythmic tension that standard 4/4 doesn't provide. **Take Five** (Brubeck) — the most famous 5/4 jazz piece; the bass vamp makes 5 feel completely natural. **Money** (Pink Floyd) — 7/4 bass riff that switches to 4/4 for the guitar solo. The key teaching point: odd meters are most successful when the riff or groove makes the meter feel inevitable rather than academic.

## Chord Extensions (9ths, 11ths, 13ths) in Songs
Extended chords add color beyond the basic triad and 7th. **What's Going On** (Marvin Gaye) opens on an Emaj9 — lush, open, sophisticated. **So What** (Miles Davis) — the "So What chord" is a stacked 4th voicing of a minor 11th. **Isn't She Lovely** (Stevie Wonder) — the harmony uses 9th chords throughout for a warm, full sound. Extensions don't change harmonic function; they add color. A ii7 with a 9th is still a ii chord — it just has more shimmer.

## Songs for Teaching Major vs. Minor
To illustrate the emotional difference between major and minor: Compare **Let It Be** (Beatles, C major — hopeful, resolved) with **Eleanor Rigby** (Beatles, E minor — melancholic, restless). Both are by the same band, same era, both iconic. The major/minor contrast couldn't be clearer. For students who ask "what does minor feel like?" — play the first 10 seconds of each back to back.

## Songs for Teaching Intervals
To teach specific intervals by ear: **Minor 2nd** — Jaws theme (John Williams). **Major 2nd** — Happy Birthday opening. **Minor 3rd** — Smoke on the Water opening riff. **Major 3rd** — Oh When the Saints Go Marching In. **Perfect 4th** — Here Comes the Bride. **Tritone** — The Simpsons theme (Danny Elfman), Purple Haze opening. **Perfect 5th** — Star Wars main theme opening (John Williams). **Minor 6th** — The Entertainer. **Major 6th** — My Bonnie Lies Over the Ocean. **Major 7th** — Take On Me (a-ha) verse. **Octave** — Somewhere Over the Rainbow opening.

## Songs for Teaching Rhythm and Groove
For syncopation and groove: **Superstition** (Stevie Wonder) — the clavinet riff lands between beats 1 and 2, creating forward momentum. **Billie Jean** (Michael Jackson) — the interplay between kick drum and bass guitar is syncopation at its most precise. **Take Five** (Brubeck) — 5/4 feels natural when the groove is this strong. For students learning to feel the beat "between the notes," these three recordings are essential listening.
